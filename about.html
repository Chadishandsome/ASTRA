Collision Prevention in Orbit

Earth’s orbit is filling up with active satellites and debris. A single collision can create thousands of fragments and trigger a chain reaction that makes key orbits unsafe for communication, navigation, and science missions. ASTRA’s goal is to spot risky encounters early and help operators avoid them.

The Problem: Crowded Orbits and Collision Risk

There are now thousands of active satellites and millions of tracked debris fragments in Earth’s orbit, especially in low Earth orbit where megaconstellations like Starlink operate. More objects mean more close approaches and a higher chance of collisions. Scientists like Donald Kessler warned that repeated impacts could lead to a cascade of debris, making some orbits unusable for long periods.

Preventing collisions is not just about tracking objects. It also needs good risk evaluation and tools that let people see what is happening in a way they can act on.

ASTRA’s Three Main Approaches

To reduce collision risk, ASTRA focuses on three connected steps: automated tracking, risk evaluation, and clear visualization for decision-makers.

1. Automated Satellite Tracking

Use radar, optical sensors, and AI-based algorithms to monitor satellites and debris and keep their orbits updated in near real time. This gives a current, accurate picture of the space environment.

Learn more about tracking

2. Risk Evaluation & Conjunction Analysis

Predict close approaches between objects, estimate collision probability, and flag the cases that are actually dangerous. This helps operators focus on the few events that really matter instead of thousands of low risk passes.

ESA on space debris risk

3. Visualization & Awareness Tools

Turn raw orbital data into interactive visual tools that show orbits, close approaches, and avoidance options. These views support operators, policymakers, and the public in understanding why certain maneuvers are needed.

Comparing the Three Approaches

Each approach tackles a different part of the collision problem. Together, they form a full workflow from raw data to real decisions.

Approach Main Goal Strengths Limitations Example Uses
Automated Tracking Maintain accurate positions for satellites and debris. Real time updates, large number of objects, better situational awareness. Needs many sensors and stable data sharing, still some uncertainty in orbits. Catalog updates, monitoring megaconstellations, detecting new debris.
Risk Evaluation & Conjunction Analysis Estimate how likely a close approach will become a collision. Filters thousands of events into a short, prioritized list of risky cases. Depends on data quality, models can be complex to tune and explain. Daily screening, maneuver decision support, reporting to mission control.
Visualization & Awareness Make collision risk understandable and visible to people. Easy to explain, supports teaching, helps brief stakeholders and the public. Does not replace detailed analysis, needs good underlying data and models. Operator dashboards, educational tools, public outreach and policy talks.
